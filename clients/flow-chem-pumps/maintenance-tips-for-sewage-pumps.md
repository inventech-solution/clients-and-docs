# Maintenance Tips for Sewage Pumps

# Maintenance Tips for Sewage Pumps

Reliable sewage pump operation depends on consistent maintenance. These tips cover the essential tasks, common failure points, and type-specific considerations for maintaining submersible sewage and drainage infrastructure.

## General Principles

**Maintenance prevents failure, it does not cause it.** The most common reason facilities defer pump maintenance is concern about disrupting operation. The reality is that unplanned failure causes far more operational disruption than scheduled maintenance, and at far greater cost.

**Compare to baseline.** Motor current draw, flow rate, and vibration level should all be measured and recorded at commissioning. Comparing current readings to baseline reveals developing problems — rising current, reduced flow, or increased vibration — before they become failures.

**Replace seals proactively.** Mechanical seals are wear items. Replace them on a schedule, not when they fail. A failed seal in a sewage application means motor replacement — many times the cost of the seal.

## Maintenance by Frequency

### Monthly checks (all pump types)
- Motor current draw — compare to nameplate and baseline; rising current indicates developing fault
- Sump/pit level — unusually high levels suggest reduced pump output
- Float switch operation — manually verify activation and cutoff points
- Cable condition — visual check for damage, kinking, or swelling

### Quarterly checks
- Intake screen — clean to remove debris accumulation
- Check valve — confirm free movement and correct seating
- Control panel — check for moisture ingress, loose terminals, indicator light function
- Insulation resistance test — 500V megohmmeter; above 1 MΩ is acceptable, below 0.5 MΩ requires investigation

### Annual service
- Mechanical seal inspection and replacement if any weeping is detected
- Impeller clearance measurement and restoration to manufacturer specification
- Bearing condition assessment
- Lubrication per manufacturer schedule (oil-filled motors)
- Full winding resistance and insulation test
- Discharge pipework and check valve inspection

## Type-Specific Maintenance

### Sewage pumps
The primary risk is solid accumulation at the intake and impeller wear from abrasive particles in wastewater. Check intake screen quarterly; check impeller clearance annually. In high-solid applications (municipal or industrial sewage with significant grit), semi-annual impeller checks may be warranted.

### Cutter pumps
In addition to standard checks, inspect the cutting mechanism at every service. Verify cutter blade sharpness — a blunt cutter increases motor load and allows solids through that the cutter should have reduced. Check for wrapped fibrous material around the cutter shaft. Blade replacement or sharpening interval depends heavily on waste stream content; establish the correct interval empirically during the first year of operation.

### Slurry and agitator pumps
Abrasive slurry accelerates wear on impeller, wear plates, and seals faster than standard sewage applications. Reduce inspection intervals accordingly — quarterly impeller clearance checks in high-abrasion applications are not excessive. Confirm agitator function at each inspection; a seized agitator allows solids to settle and reduces pump efficiency.

### Dewatering pumps
Dewatering pumps in construction applications are frequently moved between sites, exposed to grit and debris, and operated intermittently. Inspect for physical damage to housing and cable at each deployment. Clean thoroughly before storage. Run briefly on clean water before returning to service after storage to clear any sediment settled during downtime.

## Electrical Maintenance

Electrical faults are a leading cause of submersible pump failure. The electrical installation requires the same attention as the pump itself:

- **Cable condition** — inspect the full run of submersible cable, including where it passes through cable glands and where it contacts the pit structure. Abrasion damage is common at contact points.
- **Panel terminals** — check all terminals for looseness and corrosion annually. Loose terminals cause voltage drop and overheating.
- **Motor protection** — verify thermal overload relay settings match the motor's full load current. Incorrectly set overloads allow motors to run in sustained overload without tripping.
- **Phase balance** (three-phase installations) — check voltage across all three phases. Imbalance above 2% significantly reduces motor life.

## Spare Parts to Keep on Hand

For any installation where pump downtime has operational consequences, keep minimum spares:

- Mechanical seal (matched to installed pump model)
- Impeller O-rings and gasket set
- Float switch (if used)
- Submersible cable section and cable gland
