# How to Select a Submersible Pump with the Right Flow Rate and Head Height

Flow rate and head are the two numbers that determine whether a pump works in your system. Get them right and the pump runs efficiently and reliably. Get them wrong and the pump either underperforms or fails early. This guide explains how to determine both values and how to use them to select the correct pump.

## Understanding Flow Rate

Flow rate is the volume of liquid the pump must move per unit of time. It is typically expressed in litres per minute (LPM) or cubic metres per hour (m³/hr).

**Calculating required flow rate:**

For dewatering applications, estimate the peak rate at which water enters the pit or excavation — from groundwater seepage, rainfall runoff, or process input. The pump must exceed this rate to progressively lower the water level, not just keep pace with it. Add 20–30% margin.

For sewage applications, calculate peak simultaneous fixture use and convert to flow rate using IS 1172 fixture unit values.

For process applications, use actual measured or specified process flow rates from equipment datasheets.

**Common mistake:** sizing to average flow rather than peak flow. A pump that keeps up with average inflow will fall behind during peak demand, causing overflow or accumulation.

## Understanding Head

Head is the total resistance the pump must overcome to move liquid from the collection point to the discharge point. It is expressed in metres.

**Total Dynamic Head (TDH) = Static Head + Friction Head + Velocity Head**

**Static head** — the vertical distance from the water surface in the source (pit, sump, or well) to the highest point of the discharge pipe. This is the minimum head the pump must overcome regardless of pipe run.

**Friction head** — the pressure lost to friction as liquid moves through the discharge pipe. It increases with: longer pipe runs, smaller pipe diameter, more bends and fittings, and higher flow velocity. Calculate using the Hazen-Williams equation or a friction loss chart for the pipe material and diameter. A 10m static head with 50m of 50mm pipe at typical sewage flow velocities may have 4–6m of friction losses, giving a TDH of 14–16m.

**Velocity head** — typically 0.05–0.2m at normal operating velocities. Include as a small buffer.

**Add 10–15% to the calculated TDH** as a safety margin for pipe ageing, minor underestimates, and seasonal variation.

## Using the Performance Curve

Every pump manufacturer publishes a performance curve — a graph with flow rate on the horizontal axis and head on the vertical axis. The curve shows how the pump performs across its operating range.

To select correctly:
1. Calculate your required flow rate
2. Calculate your TDH
3. Plot this operating point on the performance curve
4. Select a pump whose best efficiency point (BEP) is close to this operating point

A pump operating near its BEP runs at maximum hydraulic efficiency — minimum energy per unit of output, minimum stress on the impeller and shaft bearings.

A pump operating far from its BEP — either at very high flow (low head) or very low flow (high head) — runs inefficiently, overheats, and wears faster. This is the most common specification error: selecting a pump that can technically deliver the required flow at the required head, but not near its BEP.

## Matching Pump HP to Operating Point

Once the operating point is established and a pump curve is selected, the motor HP is determined by the hydraulic power required at that point plus efficiency losses. The pump manufacturer's curve typically includes a power consumption line or table — use this rather than estimating.

Common HP ranges for typical applications:

| Application | Typical flow (m³/hr) | Typical TDH (m) | HP range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small construction dewatering | 10–30 | 5–10 | 1–2 HP |
| Medium construction / basement sewage | 30–80 | 10–20 | 2–5 HP |
| Municipal lift station | 50–200 | 10–25 | 5–15 HP |
| Industrial process | Variable | Variable | Assess per curve |
