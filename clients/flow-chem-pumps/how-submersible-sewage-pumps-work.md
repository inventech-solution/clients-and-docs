# How Submersible Sewage Pumps Work: A Comprehensive Guide

# How Submersible Sewage Pumps Work

Submersible sewage pumps move wastewater containing solids from a collection point to a higher-level discharge. Understanding how they work — and why their design differs from standard water pumps — helps in selecting the right pump and understanding what maintenance it requires.

## The Core Operating Principle

A submersible sewage pump converts rotational motor energy into hydraulic pressure using an impeller. The motor, sealed against liquid ingress, drives the impeller via a rotating shaft. As the impeller spins, it accelerates incoming liquid outward, creating a pressure differential that draws more liquid from the intake and forces it through the discharge.

The fundamental difference between a sewage pump and a standard water pump is the impeller design — engineered to handle solids without clogging — and the motor sealing system, which must tolerate a significantly more aggressive environment than clean water.

## Impeller Types for Sewage Applications

**Vortex impeller** — the most clog-resistant design for sewage. The impeller sits recessed in the pump body; the liquid passes through the pump chamber without making direct contact with the impeller. Solids pass through with the liquid rather than meeting the impeller face. This is the standard choice for raw sewage with unpredictable solid content.

**Channel (open) impeller** — one or two large channels allow solids to pass through. More hydraulically efficient than a vortex impeller but slightly more susceptible to clogging with stringy material. Used in applications where solid content is predictable and hydraulic efficiency matters.

**Cutting impeller (cutter pump)** — a hardened steel rotating cutter at the intake shreds fibrous material, rags, and solid waste before it reaches the impeller. The most reliable option where fibrous waste is routinely present. Higher first cost; significantly lower maintenance cost than a standard impeller that blocks repeatedly on fibrous waste.

## The Sealing System

The mechanical seal is the critical component that separates the pumped liquid from the motor cavity. In sewage applications, it is under constant attack from abrasive particles, aggressive chemistry, and continuous mechanical movement.

**Single mechanical seal** — one rotating face against one stationary face. Adequate for clean water. Not recommended for sewage applications where a single seal failure means immediate motor ingress.

**Double mechanical seal** — two seal faces in series with an oil-filled chamber between them. If the inner seal fails, the outer seal protects the motor until the fault is detected. This is the minimum acceptable specification for any sewage pump in continuous service.

**Seal face materials** — standard CAR/CER (carbon/ceramic) faces are adequate for low-abrasion applications. For sewage with significant suspended solids or abrasive content, SiC/SiC (silicon carbide) faces are harder and more wear-resistant.

## Motor Construction

The motor must operate continuously in a hostile environment — submerged in sewage, running at elevated temperature, potentially handling abrasive or corrosive liquid.

**Motor type** — submersible sewage pumps use either dry motors (sealed, liquid-cooled externally by surrounding liquid) or oil-filled motors (internal oil cooling). Both designs are used; dry motors require no oil changes but must maintain seal integrity; oil-filled motors require scheduled oil condition checks.

**Winding material** — copper-wound (SECW) motors have better electrical conductivity, tolerate heat better, and have longer service life than aluminium-wound equivalents under continuous load.

**Insulation class** — Class F insulation (155°C) is standard for industrial submersible sewage pumps. It handles the temperature rise from continuous operation without degradation.

**IP rating** — IP68 is required for any pump in continuous submersion. This means the motor and sealing have been tested at depth for a sustained period without ingress. Anything lower is not suitable for permanent installation.

## Solid Handling

Every submersible sewage pump has a maximum permissible solid size — the largest solid that can pass through the pump without causing blockage or damage. This is not a soft guideline; exceeding it regularly damages the pump. Match the specification to the actual waste stream.

For sewage applications in India — residential colonies, commercial buildings, municipal lift stations — the typical range is 35–70mm maximum solid size depending on model and HP rating.

## Applications

- Residential sewage collection pits and lift stations
- Commercial building wastewater (hotels, hospitals, offices below main sewer grade)
- Municipal sewage lift stations and transfer points
- Industrial effluent with moderate solid content
- STP and ETP intake pumping and transfer circuits
